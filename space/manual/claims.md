# Writing a claim

A **claim** is the moment your portfolio stops being a record and becomes an argument. Everything you have logged — CPD entries, reflections, evidence files — exists to support claims.

> **note** A claim is not a description. It is an argued statement, written for an assessor, that you meet a particular standard. It does the interpretive work that no list of activities can do.

## When you are ready to write

A claim is ready to write when you can answer **yes** to all three:

- I can name the standard or criterion code (e.g. *"1.1 Demonstrably effective individual teaching excellence"*)
- I have at least three CPD entries that, taken together, support the claim
- I can summarise in one sentence what these entries demonstrate *collectively* — something no single activity does on its own

If any of these is missing, the claim is not yet ready. Continue building CPD entries.

## What a strong claim contains

The Path claim template has four sections. Each does specific work.

### The claim itself

Two to four paragraphs, written in the **first person**, written **for an assessor**.

Three things every claim must do:

1. **State the claim clearly**, in language an assessor will recognise as addressing the standard
2. **Situate it** — over what period, at what scale, in what scope?
3. **Signal the strongest single piece of evidence**, so the assessor knows what to look for

> **tip** Write the claim sentence first. Then build the supporting paragraphs. Most weak claims are weak because the writer never wrote the sentence.

### Evidence summary

The interpretive layer. What does this body of work *collectively* demonstrate that no single piece does on its own?

> **warning** This is the section most people skip or under-write. It is also the section assessors care about most. The CPD entries show *what happened*; this section shows *what it means*.

### Evidence chain

A bulleted list of CPD entries, each with a single line saying what it contributes.

```
- [[cpd/2025-09-15-module-redesign]] — evidence of curriculum-level intervention
- [[cpd/2026-01-20-cohort-feedback]] — student-level impact, with figures
- [[cpd/2025-11-04-peer-observation]] — independent verification by senior colleague
```

> **success** When the chain is built well, an assessor can scan it in 30 seconds and understand the case.

### Gaps and development

Honest assessment. What is still thin? What counter-evidence might a sceptical reader cite, and how would you respond? What is in development that will strengthen the claim before submission?

> **danger** Do not skip this section. Assessors trust claims that name their own weaknesses. They distrust claims that pretend to be airtight.

## Forward-looking claims

Some frameworks (including UoL TSPP) include sections on **future contributions** — what you intend to do over the next 3–5 years.

These use a different template (**Future-claim** in the Capture menu). They have four sections:

- **Aspirations** — concrete plans, not a wish list. Name initiatives, partnerships, outputs.
- **Grounding in past experience** — why these plans are credible, drawing on your evidence-based claims
- **Alignment with strategy** — how the plans serve your institution's strategic priorities
- **Indicators of success** — what would tell you (and an assessor) in 2–3 years that the plans had landed

> **tip** Forward-looking claims are weakest when they read like a vision statement. They are strongest when they read like a credible project plan.
