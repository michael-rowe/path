# Choosing a reflection framework

A reflection framework is a set of prompts that structure your thinking. There is no single best one. Different frameworks suit different kinds of experience.

Path supports four. They take 30 seconds to choose between.

## Quick chooser

| If you want to… | Use |
|---|---|
| Work through an experience fully, including how it felt | **Gibbs** |
| Keep it brief and action-focused | **ERA** |
| Move from the event to its meaning to your next step | **Driscoll** |
| Bring theory and evidence into the analysis | **Rolfe** |

## Gibbs

Six stages: **description → feelings → evaluation → analysis → conclusion → action plan**.

> **note** Gibbs is the most thorough. It explicitly asks about feelings, which other models do not. Best for experiences with significant emotional content (a difficult patient interaction, a peer review you found hard to receive, a leadership decision under pressure).

A full Gibbs reflection takes 20–40 minutes to write properly. Do not start one in five minutes between meetings.

## ERA

Three stages: **Experience → Reflection → Action**.

> **tip** ERA is the lightest. Best when you have one specific lesson and one specific action, and you do not need to dwell. Good for routine but worthwhile activities — a workshop, a shadowing session, a methodology you tried.

A full ERA reflection takes 5–15 minutes.

## Driscoll

Three stages framed as questions: **What? → So what? → Now what?**.

> **note** Driscoll is the most conversational. Good for reflective dialogue (with a supervisor, a mentor, or yourself). The questions are deliberately broad so you can take the reflection in the direction it most needs to go.

## Rolfe

Three stages, same names as Driscoll but with different intent: **What? (descriptive) → So what? (theoretical) → Now what? (action)**.

> **tip** Rolfe explicitly asks for **theory and evidence** in the second stage. Best when the experience connects to a body of literature, a clinical model, or an institutional framework you want to interrogate. The most academic of the four.

## A worked example: choosing for a single event

You taught a session that did not go well. Halfway through, you realised the room was disengaged.

- **Gibbs** would ask how it felt — useful if part of what you need to process is the emotional weight.
- **ERA** would skip straight to *"what did the experience show me, and what will I try next time?"* — useful if you already know the lesson.
- **Driscoll** would ask *"so what does this say about my teaching?"* — useful if the meaning is what you need to work out.
- **Rolfe** would invite you to draw on educational theory (cognitive load, andragogy, formative assessment) — useful for an academic reflection on practice.

> **success** Pick the framework that matches the question you most need to ask. Do not pick the longest one to look thorough.

## How to start a reflection

1. Click **Capture something** (or `Ctrl-Alt-c`)
2. Pick **Reflection**
3. Pick a framework
4. Replace `short-description` in the suggested page name with what the reflection is about (e.g. `attendance-pattern-AHP12345`)
5. Click OK and write
